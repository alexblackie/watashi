Topre Realforce 104UW:
A Review

I recently purchased a Topre Realforce keyboard. It was a considerable investment which I justified through brief but first-hand experience. I had known about Topre keyboards for a while, even owning the low-end CoolerMaster Novatouch for a time. [Ultimately, the Novatouch's switches didn't feel that spectacular, and I happily returned to my Cherry MX Browns.] My perspectives were changed forever, however, once I tried a friend's weighted Realforce.

I was pointed to EliteKeyboards by the same friend, who had noticed my beloved beige-coloured model was back in-stock. I completed the purchase just a couple minutes afterwards. I anxiously awaited the Canada Post delivery person for just over a week, and finally my baby arrived.

At the time of writing, I have been heavily using the keyboard for nearly two weeks. I have written thousands of words of English prose, and an even greater amount of code. For the first few days of ownership, I didn't want to stop typing. As you can perhaps assume from this blog post, I still don't want to stop typing.

I feel I must at least mention the beige. I haven't had a beige computer or computer peripheral since my first computer—a Compaq Deskpro running Windows 98. A wonderful machine in its beige-painted steel enclosure, supporting a beige 14" CRT monitor, attached to a beige keyboard itself beside a beige mouse. I look around my desk now and all I see is black-on-black-on-black…

In white, beige, or other colours, peripherals have character. I miss the days when my desk had character. Now it's just a pile of drab black squares.

Therefore, I was very excited when ordering this keyboard to finally be able to own something manufactured present-day in beige. It is stark amongst my otherwise black-themed desk and I love it.

The most important aspect of a keyboard is how it feels to use. For a keyboard at this pricepoint, the expectations are naturally set quite high. Topre really delivers, however, and even having tried one before, I was still taken by the feeling of the keyboard.

Each keypress starts with a sharp drop, as the high-placed bump forces you to bottom out every time. This crisp, high bump is met with a nice soft landing however, so it doesn't feel harsh. With the release, the key springs immediately back up with you, making a satisfying muddy-sounding pop as it reaches its initial position. The combination of softness, springy response, and crisp actuation makes for an extremely comfortable and satisfying switch.

Not only do the switches feel great, but they're individually weighted to account for your weaker fingers. Your pinky finger likely isn't as strong as your index finger, and so the keys get gradually lighter towards the edges of the keyboard. Additionally, the non-alphanumeric keys are even heavier, which gives them a very "chunky" feeling; even more satisfying and crisp than the normal keys.

Using my previous keyboard with Cherry MX Brown switches, they feel almost linear in comparison. The bump is significantly less pronounced than Topre switches, and the harsh bottoming-out feels painful (though that could likely be solved with some O-rings).

Interestingly, I have a new appreciation for my Thinkpad's keyboard after experiencing Topre switches. The Thinkpad keyboards manage to maintain a similarly-satisfying bump, and transitioning between my laptop and my Realforce isn't a jarring experience. I do have to credit IBM & Lenovo for their fantastic keyboards; I always loved my Thinkpad keyboard, and now I know why. It's essentially just a shallower, not quite as springy version of a Topre switch.

There is only one thing I dislike about this keyboard. It is huge.

This thing is a whale posing as a HID. The huge forehead is seemingly without purpose, the numpad is a feature I have learned to forget, and it has a surprising weight.

Luckily, I don't carry it around with me so the weight is not an issue; I mostly live in the Terminal so mouse distance isn't a huge problem; and my desk is not want for space so the large footprint doesn't cause any headaches.

In other words, my only issues with this keyboard are superficial.

I couldn't be happier with this keyboard. Its typing experience is far above anything else I have used, and its beige strikes a wonderful aesthetic contrast in the standard dark colourscheme of modern technology.

Its price leaves the wallet bruised and beaten, but I can't see myself coming to regret purchasing this keyboard any time soon. It may cost twice as much as my previous keyboard, but I would consider it twice as enjoyable to use. I haven't enjoyed typing this much since getting my first mechanical keyboard all those years ago.

After all that, would I recommend this keyboard? The answer is: maybe. I adore mine, but its hefty price tag means it's not something most people will impulse-buy just to try out. I would recommend making friends with someone who owns one to try it first, or diving in blind only if you have the cash to spend and are dissatisfied with your current keyboard.

I may even recommend trying "mainstream" mechanical keyboards first, if you haven't. A lot of people settle on Cherry MX switches and never have any reason to go elsewhere.

I absolutely do not recommend getting "cheap" Topre keyboards either. The CoolerMaster NovaTouch, and even Topre's own TypeHeaven are simply not comparable to the experience of the Realforce.
